Planets

Planets are heavenly bodies revolving around the sun in the elliptical orbits. They do not emit light of their own, but they appear bright as they reflect light of sun. These are eight in number. They can be seen with a small telecope or binoculars.
According to International Astronomical Union in 2006, a planet must have three basic properties:

  1. It must revolve around a star.
  2. It must be big enough to have enough gravity to force it into a spherical shape.
  3. It must be big enough that its gravity cleared away any other objects of a similar size near its orbit around the Sun.

Uniqueness of Planets

According to Nebular hypothesis, every star, planet and moon are made from nebular cloud. The stars were formed at the first before planets and asteriods. The planets are formed 4.5 billion years ago. Nebular clouds are made of helium, hydrogen and dust particles. When these clouds were rotating, helium and hydrogen started gathering at the centre. At the centre, these molecules collided with one another. Due to collisions and fractions, fusion reaction took place and the Sun was created. After that, heavier elements like oxygen, nitrogen, carbon, and so forth moved away from the centre. These elements started forming nebular clouds, and ultimately, the planets were formed with further rotations, collisions and fractions.
Due to their higher boiling points, only metals and silicates could exist in solid form closer to the Sun, and these would eventually form the terrestrial planets of Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars. Because metallic elements only comprised a very small fraction of the solar nebula, the terrestrial planets could not grow very large. In contrast, the giant planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune) formed beyond the point between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter where material is cool enough for volatile icy compounds to remain solid. This is the uniqueness of the planets.
